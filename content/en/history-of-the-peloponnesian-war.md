History of the Peloponnesian War by Thucydides is one of the most influential and enduring works of classical antiquity and a foundational text in the tradition of Western historiography. Written in the late fifth century BCE by an Athenian general who personally experienced the conflict, the work provides a detailed and systematic account of the long and devastating war between Athens and Sparta. Thucydides examines not only the major military campaigns and battles, but also the political negotiations, shifting alliances, and internal struggles within the Greek city-states that shaped the course of the war.

Unlike many earlier historians, Thucydides deliberately rejects mythological explanations and the involvement of the gods. Instead, he adopts a rational and analytical approach, emphasizing cause and effect, human decision-making, and the influence of power, fear, and self-interest on political behavior. He is particularly attentive to speeches, which he reconstructs to reflect the arguments and motivations of key figures, offering insight into the ideological conflicts between democracy, oligarchy, and imperial ambition.

As a result, History of the Peloponnesian War is often regarded as the first true attempt at scientific or critical historiography. Its sober tone and unflinching portrayal of human suffering reveal the moral and psychological consequences of war, including corruption, brutality, and the collapse of traditional values.
